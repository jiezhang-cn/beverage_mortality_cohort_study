# Multimodal population study reveals beverage consumption patterns and differential association with mortality risk and multi-organ health
 
Beverage consumption is an essential component of the daily diet, yet how beverage consumption patterns differentially affect organ-specific health and overall mortality risk, and what constitutes optimal fluid intake from beverages, remain largely unclear. Moving beyond analysis of individual beverages, we applied data-driven approaches in 207,592 UK Biobank participants to identify four distinct beverage patterns, comprising three dominant patterns (coffee/tea-milk, sugar-sweetened and artificially sweetened beverages [SSB-ASB], and water-juice) and a mixed pattern. Compared with the water-juice pattern, the coffee/tea-milk pattern was associated with a 8% lower risk of all-cause mortality, whereas the SSB-ASB pattern was associated with a 10% higher mortality risk. Phenome-wide association analyses revealed that the SSB-ASB pattern was associated with adverse multi-organ health profiles spanning renal, cardiovascular, adiposity, pulmonary and brain phenotypes, whereas the coffee/tea-milk pattern showed more favorable cardiac, renal and metabolic but less favorable arterial and pulmonary outcomes. Finally, we estimated a personalized fluid intake achievement rate incorporating individuals’ environmental conditions, anthropometric characteristics, physical activity, and actual total fluid intake. This metric showed a U-shaped association with overall mortality, but with striking heterogeneity across beverage patterns: greater fluid intake achievement rate was paradoxically associated with progressively higher mortality risk in the SSB-ASB pattern, but lower mortality risk in the water-juice pattern. Our findings underscore the importance of meeting personalized fluid needs within healthy beverage patterns for long-term health.
